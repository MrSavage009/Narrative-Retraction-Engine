## I. The CEM as Theoretical Primitive

The Controlled Epistemic Microenvironment is not a "test case." It is the **smallest unit of discourse for which algorithmic correctness is formally decidable and humanly verifiable**. Its purpose is not to exercise code paths but to **make the mapping from mathematical structure to semantic consequence explicit and inspectable**.

Every CEM instantiates a **provable proposition** about the relationship between cost topology and optimal strategy. The Cascade Cover-Up proves: *When entailment chains are longer than contradiction edges, upstream retraction dominates under modular costs but not under submodular costs with high leaf damage.* This is not an empirical claim. It is a **theorem encoded in graph structure**. The algorithm's job is to rediscover what the CEM already encodes.

This reframes validation entirely. A CEM is not "passed" or "failed." It is **read**—like a proof, like a circuit diagram, like a controlled experiment in particle physics where the expected result is computed from first principles before the apparatus is switched on.

## II. The Six Invariants of CEM Design

### Invariant 1: Semantic Necessity

Every node, edge, and weight must be **justified by the narrative corpus**. No synthetic padding. If a node exists, a speaker in the thread uttered it or it follows deductively from what was uttered. This ensures the primary/derived distinction is not an artifact of graph construction but an **observable property of discourse**.

In the Cascade Cover-Up, p3 ("Your negligence caused the entire cascade") is derived because Alice never says it in isolation—it is the logical consequence of p1 and p2 combined. The extractor must recognize this. If it cannot, the CEM has exposed a **failure mode in extraction**, not in optimization.

### Invariant 2: Topological Completeness

A CEM must contain every motif that the preprocessing layers are designed to handle:

| Motif | Tests | Diagnostic Output |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| **Convergence funnel** (p1,p2→p3) | Closure union logic, overlapping path counting | Greedy must evaluate shared marginal gain correctly |
| **Diamond branch** (p3→p4/p5 and p3→p7) | Heuristic branching, independent subproblem decomposition | RC-A* must explore both branches without double-counting |
| **Circular entailment cluster** (p4↔p5) | SCC condensation, closure inheritance | Meta-node must inherit union, not sum, of member closures |
| **Bypass bridge** (p9 independent to a3) | Multi-source min-cut heuristic | Heuristic must see disjoint paths; h > 0 required |
| **Epistemic orphan** (p10, p11) | Gamma-term observability | General solver must diverge from submodular |

A CEM missing any motif is **incomplete**—not merely simple, but **theoretically insufficient**.

### Invariant 3: Cost Orthogonality

The cost calibration profile must create **non-aligned incentives** across solver classes. This requires three independent pressure axes:

- **α-pressure** (structural): Favors few, large closures. Upstream cuts dominate.
- **β-pressure** (damage): Favors avoiding high-damage nodes. Leaf cuts may dominate if upstream damage is severe.
- **γ-pressure** (residual): Favors emptying the board. Orphans become liabilities.

The calibration is correct when each pair of pressures produces a **different Pareto-optimal protocol**. In our CEM:

| Cost Class | α | β | γ | Optimal Primary Retraction | Mechanism |
|------------|---|---|---|---------------------------|-----------|
| Modular | 1 | 0 | 0 | {p1, p2, p7, p9} | Minimize total weight; upstream absorption wins |
| Submodular | 1 | 1 | 0 | {p1, p2, p3, p9} | p3's damage (1.0) cheaper than p7+p8's damage (10.5) |
| General | 1 | 1 | 1.5 | {p1, p2, p3, p9, p10, p11} | γ forces orphan retraction |

If two solvers agree, the calibration has failed. The CEM must be **rejected and redesigned**.

### Invariant 4: Human Verifiability

The optimal solution for each cost class must be **computable by a domain expert in under five minutes** without algorithmic assistance. This is not a usability requirement. It is a **validity requirement**: if the expected output is not knowable independently, the CEM cannot discriminate between correct algorithms and plausible-looking errors.

Verifiability requires:
- **Small node count** (|S| ≤ 15, |P| ≤ 8)
- **Transparent damage semantics** (damage scores map to intuitive "harshness" of claims)
- **Linear entailment chains** (no exponential path counting)

### Invariant 5: Extractor Fidelity

The CEM must be **extractable by the intended pipeline**. If the extractor is an LLM prompted for entailment edges, the CEM's narrative must use **explicit cue phrases** ("because," "therefore," "proves that") that the extractor is trained to recognize. Implicit enthymemes—arguments with suppressed premises—belong in stress tests, not in baseline CEMs.

### Invariant 6: Minimal Non-Determinism

Every element of the CEM must be **fully specified**. No random weights, no sampled edges, no stochastic extraction. The CEM is a **mathematical object**, not a distribution. Randomness belongs in robustness tests (ensembles of CEMs), never in the CEM itself.
